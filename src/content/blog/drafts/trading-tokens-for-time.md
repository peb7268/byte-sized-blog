A few months ago I caught myself pricing out a service to do a chore I do every week. Twenty minutes, give or take. Tedious, low-stakes, the kind of thing you resent more each time. The going rate to outsource it was real money, every month, forever. I almost paid it.

Then it hit me that I was about to rent a human to do something I could teach an agent to do once — for roughly the cost of a fancy coffee in tokens.

That moment reframed the entire agentic shift for me. We keep talking about agents as a coding-speed story: write features faster, ship more, review the output. That's the [up-the-stack](/blog/intelligence-moves-up-the-stack/) version, and it's true. But there's a quieter, more personal version that I think matters more in daily life, and almost nobody is naming it out loud:

> Tokens are cheap and racing toward free. Your time and attention are not, and never will be. The whole game is learning when to spend the cheap thing to buy back the expensive one.

That's the trade. **Trading tokens for time.** And once you see it, you can't unsee it.

## The old options were all bad

Think about how we've historically dealt with the mundane work that clogs a life. You had three moves, and all of them cost you the scarce resource.

You could **do it yourself** — pay in hours, the one currency you can never earn back. You could **hire it out** — a nanny, an assistant, a service, a contractor — and pay in money *and* in the overhead of managing another person. Or you could **just let it rot** — pay in the low-grade stress of a thing that never gets done.

For most of human history those were the only three doors. The agentic shift quietly added a fourth: **build an agent. Automate the workflow.** Pay once, in tokens and a little setup time, and the thing does itself from then on.

What makes this genuinely new isn't that automation exists — we've had scripts and cron jobs for decades. It's that the cost of *creating* the automation collapsed. You no longer need to be a programmer to wire up a workflow. You need to be able to describe what you want clearly, which — not coincidentally — is the [exact skill](/blog/intelligence-moves-up-the-stack/) the whole agentic era rewards. The barrier that used to make "just automate it" a punchline for non-engineers is gone.

## A decision frame that fits on a napkin

Not everything should be automated, and I'll get to the honest caveats. But you need a way to decide fast, because the failure mode is spending an afternoon automating something you do twice a year. Here's the frame I use. Three factors, multiplied:

**Frequency × Time-cost × Delegability.**

- **Frequency** — How often does this recur? Daily and weekly things are gold. Once-a-quarter things almost never are.
- **Time-cost** — How long does each instance take, including the *switching* cost of dropping what you were doing to handle it? A two-minute task that yanks you out of deep focus can cost twenty.
- **Delegability** — How cleanly can the task be specified? If you can write down the steps, an agent can probably run them. If it requires taste, relationships, or judgment that lives only in your head, it resists delegation — for now.

Multiply those three. A high score — frequent, expensive, cleanly specifiable — is a screaming candidate. A low score on any one factor usually kills it. The rare quarterly task with deep judgment? Do it yourself. The daily, mechanical, fully-specifiable chore? You're leaving hours on the table by *not* building the agent.

The multiplication matters more than any single term. People fixate on time-cost ("it only takes five minutes") and miss that five minutes times every weekday times forever is a part-time job you're working for free.

## The mundane stuff actually worth it

Concrete is better than abstract here, so here's the texture of what clears the bar — none of it glamorous, which is the point:

- **The weekly digest you assemble by hand.** Pulling status from three tools, reformatting it, pasting it somewhere. Frequent, mechanical, specifiable. Textbook.
- **Inbox and message triage.** Not *answering* — answering needs you — but sorting, flagging, surfacing the three things that actually need a human and burying the forty that don't.
- **The recurring report nobody enjoys.** Same shape every time, different numbers. If the shape is stable, the assembly is delegable.
- **Personal admin with a clear rulebook.** Categorizing expenses, renaming and filing documents, the small bureaucratic upkeep of being an adult. Boring, rule-bound, perfect.
- **Research fan-out.** "Go find me the five best sources on X and summarize the disagreements." High time-cost, very delegable, and you stay in the loop on the part that needs judgment — deciding what to *do* with the answer.

Notice the pattern. The agent does the **assembly and the grunt-sorting**; you keep the **decision**. That's not an accident — it's the same division of labor that shows up everywhere in this shift, and the same one I keep circling in [the review is the work now](/blog/the-review-is-the-work-now/). The machine generates and gathers. You judge. The mundane half is exactly the half that was never a good use of you.

## The honest caveats

I'd be selling you something if I pretended this is free, so here's the part the hype skips.

**Setup cost is real, and it's front-loaded.** The trade is "pay once now, save forever," but the "pay once" is non-trivial the first few times you do it. Your first agent will take longer to build than the chore would've taken to just *do*. The math only works if the thing recurs enough to amortize that. This is precisely why the frequency term in the frame is doing so much work — it's what turns a one-time cost into a long-term win.

**Maintenance isn't zero.** An agent is not a fire-and-forget appliance. The world it touches changes — a website moves a button, a format shifts, an API deprecates — and your automation breaks quietly at the worst moment. Every agent you build is a small ongoing liability, not just an asset. Build ten thoughtless ones and you've created a second job maintaining your time-savers. Be selective. A few robust, high-value agents beat a sprawl of fragile ones.

**Not everything should be automated, and some things shouldn't be delegated at all.** Some tasks *are* the thinking. The friction of doing them yourself is where you actually understand the problem — automate those away and you've offloaded the judgment along with the labor, which is the [exact trap](/blog/the-review-is-the-work-now/) that hollows people out. And some tasks are relational: the handwritten note, the hard conversation, the thing whose entire value is that *you* did it. Optimizing those is missing the point of having a life.

The frame protects you here too. Low delegability isn't only "the agent can't do it" — sometimes it's "the agent *shouldn't*, because the doing is the value."

## The reframe worth keeping

Here's where I've landed. The agentic shift is usually sold as a productivity story — do more, faster. I think that undersells it. At the personal level it's a **reallocation** story. The cheapest resource on earth right now is a competent agent's output, and it's getting cheaper every quarter. The most expensive, permanently non-renewable resource you own is your own attention. For the first time, there's a clean exchange rate between them.

So the question to keep asking, every time you feel that flicker of resentment at a recurring chore, isn't "can I afford to automate this?" It's the inverse: *can I afford to keep paying for this in the only currency I can't get back?*

Spend the cheap thing. Buy back the expensive one. That's the trade — and I'd argue it's the most underrated move of this whole era. This is also, quietly, the personal-scale version of the bet I make at the [systems level](/blog/pcms-manifesto/): build the machine that does the work, and spend your finite attention on the machine, not the chore.

What's the most mundane thing you've handed to an agent — and what did you do with the hours you got back? That second half is the part I actually care about.
